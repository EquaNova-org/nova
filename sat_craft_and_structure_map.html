Sub-type 1 Words in context ✕ close
These questions give a word or short phrase from the passage and ask what it most nearly means in context. They do not test dictionary definitions — they test how the word is used in that specific sentence and passage.
"As used in the text, what does the word 'charged' most nearly mean?"
Strategy: Substitute each answer choice back into the sentence and see which preserves the original meaning. Eliminate choices that are valid definitions but don't fit context.
★ The most common trap is picking the most familiar meaning — the SAT almost always uses the word in a less obvious sense.
Sub-type 2 Text structure & purpose ✕ close
These ask why an author wrote something a certain way — the purpose of the whole passage, a paragraph, or a specific sentence. They also test whether students can identify how a text is structured (problem–solution, compare–contrast, claim–evidence).
"Which choice best describes the overall structure of the text?"
Strategy: Ask: what does the author do in the first half vs the second half? The structure mirrors the author's purpose — presenting a problem then a solution, or making a claim then supporting it.
★ An answer that's factually accurate but misses the tone or intent is usually wrong. Both content and approach must match.
Sub-type 3 Cross-text connections ✕ close
These pair two short passages (Text 1 and Text 2) on a related topic and ask how they relate — agreement, disagreement, one extending the other, or each addressing a different aspect of the same subject.
"Based on the texts, how would the author of Text 2 most likely respond to the claim made in Text 1?"
Strategy: Summarise each text's main point in one sentence before answering, then determine the relationship. The correct answer must be grounded in both texts.
★ Wrong answers often reference only one text, or exaggerate the degree of agreement or disagreement between the two authors.
