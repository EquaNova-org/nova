Sub-type 1 Rhetorical synthesis ✕ close
These are unique to the digital SAT. The student is given a set of bullet-point notes (as if taken by a researcher or student) and must choose the answer that best synthesises those notes to accomplish a specific goal — for example, "to emphasise a contrast" or "to introduce the study to a general audience."

Both the content (what information is included) and the rhetoric (how it's framed) must match the stated goal.
"The student wants to emphasise the unexpected nature of the finding. Which choice most effectively uses relevant information from the notes to accomplish this goal?"
Strategy: First, identify the exact goal in the question. Then eliminate answers that include irrelevant notes or frame the information in a way that doesn't match the goal.
★ Wrong answers often include accurate information from the notes but miss the rhetorical goal (e.g., they summarise instead of emphasising contrast).
Sub-type 2 Transitions ✕ close
These present two sentences or a sentence with a blank and ask which transition word or phrase best connects the ideas. The relationship between the two ideas determines the right transition — contrast, addition, cause-effect, example, concession, etc.
"Which choice completes the text with the most logical transition?"
Strategy: Before looking at the options, read both sentences and ask yourself — does the second idea agree, disagree, add to, or result from the first? Then pick the transition that matches that relationship.
★ Many students choose a transition that sounds sophisticated (e.g., "nonetheless") without checking whether the logical relationship actually calls for contrast. Always match the relationship first.
