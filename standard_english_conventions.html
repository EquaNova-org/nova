Sub-type 1 Boundaries
Boundary questions test whether students can correctly punctuate sentences — especially the borders between independent clauses, dependent clauses, and phrases. The core skill is recognising what kind of grammatical unit each part of the sentence is, then applying the right punctuation rule.

These cover: run-on sentences, comma splices, sentence fragments, and how to use punctuation marks to separate or join clauses correctly.
Period / semicolonJoins two independent clauses as separate sentences
Comma + conjunctionJoins two independent clauses (FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so)
Colon / dashIntroduces an explanation, list, or elaboration after a complete clause
Comma aloneCannot join two independent clauses — that's a comma splice
"Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?"
Strategy: Identify what comes before and after the blank. Is each part an independent clause? A dependent clause? A phrase? Match the punctuation to the grammatical relationship.
⭑ Key Trap The comma splice is the single most tested error in this category — two independent clauses joined by only a comma. Learn to spot it instantly.
Sub-type 2 Form, Structure & Sense
These test a wider range of grammar rules focused on choosing the correct word form to make the sentence grammatically accurate and clear. If Boundaries is about punctuation, this sub-type is about everything else in grammar.
Subject-verb agreementVerb must match its subject in number, not the nearest noun
Verb tense & moodTense must be consistent and logical within the passage's timeline
Pronoun agreementPronoun must match its antecedent in number and gender
ModifiersDescriptive phrases must be placed next to what they describe
Parallel structureItems in a list or comparison must use the same grammatical form
Possessives vs pluralsApostrophe placement determines meaning (its vs it's, etc.)
"Which choice completes the text so that it conforms to the conventions of Standard English?"
Strategy: Look at what the question is really testing — identify the subject, verb, pronoun, or modifier in question, then apply the relevant rule. Don't rely on "what sounds right."
⭑ Key Trap Subject-verb agreement errors often hide by placing a long phrase between the subject and verb (e.g., "The results of the study was..." — the subject is "results," not "study").

Category Strategy

Why this category is learnable

Grammar rules are consistent and finite. Unlike reading comprehension, which varies by passage, the same rules appear every test. Learn a rule once — comma splice, subject-verb agreement, modifier placement — and you gain reliable points every time.

Key habits to build

  • Label clause types: independent or dependent?
  • Find the true subject (ignore prepositional phrases)
  • Don't rely on "sounds right" — apply the rule
  • For modifier questions: who/what is doing the action?
⭑ Note Standard English Conventions accounts for ~26% of the R&W section. Many students find grammar questions improve fastest with targeted practice — the rules are explicit and learnable, making this category an excellent focus for score improvement in a short time.
